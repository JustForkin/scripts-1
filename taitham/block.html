This page lists characters in the following Unicode block and provides information about them.

  • Tai Tham

This is not authoritative, peer-reviewed information – these are just notes I have gathered and copied from various places.

For a summary of the script and its use in writing systems, see the page Tai Tham script summary. For similar information related to other scripts, see this list.

We have usage data for no languages that use the Tai Tham script.

Find

If you click on any red example text, you will see at the bottom right of the page a list of the characters that make up the example.

Read more about how to use this page.

To find a character by codepoint, type #char0000 at the end of the URL in the address bar, where 0000 is a four-figure, hex codepoint number, all in uppercase. Or type the character or the hex number in the Find control above.

To view this page as intended, you need a Tai Tham font. This page comes with a Tai Tham webfont. Click the blue vertical bar at the bottom right of the page to apply other fonts, if you have them on your system. For transcriptions I recommend the excellent and free Doulos SIL font. The large character in the box will not be rendered unless the webfont downloaded with the page or a system font has a glyph for it. (If there is no glyph and you want to see what it looks like, click on See in UniView.)

Information about languages that use these characters is taken from the list maintained for the Character Use app. The list is not exhaustive.

References are indicated by superscript characters. Wherever possible, those contain direct links to the source material. When such a pointer is alongside an arrow → it means that it's worth following the link for the additional information it provides. Digits refer to the main sources, which are listed at the bottom of a set of notes.

When you are using UniView and you turn on Show notes, UniView will pull in information about characters from this page.

(See also  ᩙ [U+1A59 TAI THAM CONSONANT SIGN FINAL NGA​].)

Tai Khün -r

Used as a final r. Khün shape for this character (shown with ka): ᨠᩘ.

The superscript consonants are limited to final r (᩺ [U+1A7A TAI THAM SIGN RA HAAM​]) and ŋ (this character) in syllables where a subscript vowel prevents the use of a subscript final consonant. Superscript forms are mainly found in handwritten text, whereas regular forms of these consonants in postscript position are the norm for printed texts. o

General sources: Everson, Owen

U+1A5A TAI THAM CONSONANT SIGN LOW PA

See in UniView

Special form p̆

Tai Lü p

The normal rendering of kᵃp͓̄p̄ʰᵃ would be ᨠᨻ᩠ᨽ, but in some Tai Lü words this sign is used instead, eg. ᨠᨽᩚ,e to move the stack upwards. Note, however, that this changes the typing order of the consonants, since a combining character has to be typed after the base. The transliteration now becomes kᵃp̄ʰp̆ᵃ. e

General sources: Everson, Owen

ᩛ

U+1A5B TAI THAM CONSONANT SIGN HIGH RATHA OR LOW PA

See in UniView

Special form ᵽ

Northern Thai p, tʰ

Has two readings.

It represents ᨮ [U+1A2E TAI THAM LETTER HIGH RATHA] in ᩈᨱᩛᩣ᩠ᨶ sᵃṇ̱ᵽā͓ṉ shape, and ᩁᨭᩛᨷᩣ᩠ᩃ rᵃṭᵽᵃbā͓ḻ government. (Compare with the rare normal subjoined form, eg. ᩈᨳᨷᩢ᩠ᨶ‍ᩁᩣᨩᨽᩢ᩠ᨮ‍ᨩ᩠ᩀᨦ‍ᩉ᩠ᨾᩱ᩵ sᵃtʰᵃbá͓ṉ‍ rāc̱p̄ʰá͓ṭʰ‍ c̱͓yᵃṉ̇‍ aʲh͓m̱¹ Chiang Mai College of Further Education.)e

It represents ᨻ [U+1A3B TAI THAM LETTER LOW PA] in ᩋᨾᩛ ‘ᵃm̱ᵽᵃ mango and ᩈᨻᩛ sᵃp̄ᵽᵃ omniscience. (Compare with the somewhat rare subjoined form, eg. ᨷᩢᨱ᩠ᨻᨷᩩᩁᩩᩇ báṇ̱͓ᵃp̄buruṣ disciple.)e

Khün p, tʰ

Khun uses ᨭᩛ, which is ᨭ +  ᩛ [U+1A2D TAI THAM LETTER RATA + U+1A5B TAI THAM CONSONANT SIGN HIGH RATHA OR LOW PA​] instead of ᨮ [U+1A2E TAI THAM LETTER HIGH RATHA].e

General sources: Everson, Owen

U+1A74 TAI THAM SIGN MAI KANG

See in UniView

Dependent vowel ō

Northern Thai -ŋ

Everson says that this diacritic is combined with  ᩣ [U+1A63 TAI THAM VOWEL SIGN AA​] or  ᩤ [U+1A64 TAI THAM VOWEL SIGN TALL AA​] to create the sound am, and that regardless of visual placement, this character should come after the vowel sign (in the same way as it does in Khmer, eg. កាំ).

It goes on to say that Northern Thai follows the Thai model, which places the mai kang to the left of the vowel (overlapping the preceding consonant), whereas Khün and Tai Lü render it over the vowel sign. e

U+1A75 TAI THAM SIGN TONE-1

See in UniView

Tone mark ¹

Northern Thai, Tai Lü

If there is a vowel over a consonant or consonant stack, the tone mark follows the vowel in storage, and is displayed above the vowel.

Otherwise, the tone is input after the consonant, ie. before a vowel sign that is displayed to the right or below, and appears over the consonant. e

Virama ͓

Northern Thai, Khün, Tai Lü

Used to cause stacking of consonants. Note that, unlike most other Brahmi scripts, this doesn't necessarily kill the vowel between the consonants.

Also unusually, it can also be used after vowel signs, and digits.

Silencer mark / final n ˟

Northern Thai

Used in Northern Thai to indicate that the character or characters it follows are not sounded. The precise range of characters not to be sounded is not defined (that is, it’s a reading rule), although it does not extend beyond one cluster. e

Tai Lü -n

Used as a final n.

Tai Khün -r

Used as a final r.

The superscript consonants are limited to final r (this character) and ŋ ( ᩘ [U+1A58 TAI THAM SIGN MAI KANG LAI​]) in syllables where a subscript vowel prevents the use of a subscript final consonant. ᩻

U+1A7B TAI THAM SIGN MAI SAM

See in UniView

Repetition / disambiguation mark ʻ

Northern Thai

Used at the end of a word to indicate that it should be repeated, eg. ᨲ᩵ᩣ᩠ᨦ taŋ to be different, vs. ᨲ᩵ᩣ᩠ᨦ᩻ taŋtaŋ different in my view. e

Also used to indicate 'double-acting' consonants, stored where the consonant would be stored if there were a separate consonant used, eg. compare ᩮᨡᩢ᩶ᩣᨡᩬᨦ ekʰá²ākʰōṉ̇ kʰawkʰoŋ belongings with an alternative spelling using this diacritic, ᨡᩢ᩶᩻ᩬᨦ kʰá²ʻōṉ̇. e

A third use is somewhat different. A subjoined letter may be medial, final, or the start of a new syllable. Mai sam can be used to indicate that a consonant begins a new syllable, in which case it is stored following the subjoined form and indicates that it is the consonant that starts the new syllable, eg. compare ᨳᩫ᩠ᨶᩁ tʰö͓ṉrᵃ tʰonra with ᨳᩫ᩠ᨶ᩻ᩁ tʰö͓ṉʻr tʰanon path (final r is pronounced as n). e
